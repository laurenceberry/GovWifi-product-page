Get started

Understand how GOV.UK Verify works

GOV.UK Verify provides a ‘hub’ between services and ‘certified companies’ who can verify users' identities. Certified companies are organisations that have met government and industry standards to provide identity assurance services as part of GOV.UK Verify. These certified companies are:

  • Barclays
  • CitizenSafe
  • Digidentity
  • Experian
  • Post Office
  • Royal Mail
  • SecureIdentity

When a user tries to access your service:

  1. Your service sends them to the GOV.UK Verify hub.
  2. The hub sends them to a certified company.
  3. The certified company sends them back to the hub.
  4. The hub sends them back to your service.

This approach means:

  • users’ data isn’t stored in one place
  • services don’t know which certified company the user chooses
  • certified companies don’t know which service the user is trying to access

What your users see

  1. Users will be asked if they already have an account with one of the certified companies.
  2. Users who already have an account just need to sign in.
  3. Users who don’t have an account can sign up online by answering questions only they know the answers to or by scanning their identity documents, for example a passport.
  4. Users will be sent straight back to your service.

See what the GOV.UK Verify user journey looks like when users view or share their driving licence information.

What your service receives

  1. The certified company will send you the user’s name, address and date of birth.
  2. You can match the user’s name, address and date of birth against records you already have, or create a new record to recognise your user in the future.

Which ‘level of assurance’

If you need to match a user to a real person, GOV.UK Verify provides different ‘levels of assurance’ (LOA) that describe how confident you need to be that your user is who they say they are. The most common ones are LOA 1 and LOA 2.

The LOA 1 user journey is simpler and faster than the LOA 2 user journey but offers a lower level of certainty that a user is who they say they are. So when you decide which LOA to use, you should think about the potential risk to your service and the person the user’s claiming to be.

Different parts of your service may need a different LOA.

LOA 1

Use LOA 1 when there wouldn’t be any serious consequences if a user impersonated someone else.

For example, if someone falsely applies for a local resident discount for a toll crossing, it wouldn’t seriously impact the real resident. The real resident can still apply for their own discount, which will overwrite the previous application.

LOA 2

LOA 2 is usually appropriate if you’ll accept the user is who they say they are on the balance of probabilities.

LOA 2 is probably right for you if:

  • there would be a serious consequence to a user impersonating someone else on your service
  • you need to be able to take users to court if they’ve been fraudulent

For example, if someone falsely submits a tax return in another person's name, that person could:

  • get a bill for money they don’t owe
  • have to pay a fine
  • spend time sorting out the mistake
